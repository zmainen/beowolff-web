How the Machine Sees Art

Two neural networks, two ways of understanding a painting

The Art Explorer maps nearly 690,000 artworks into a two-dimensional landscape where similar works land near each other. But what does "similar" mean? The answer depends on which network is doing the looking.

We use two complementary approaches — one that understands what a work depicts and what tradition it belongs to, and one that understands how a work feels. Together, they capture something closer to the full dimensionality of aesthetic experience.

SigLIP (Google, 2023) is a vision-language model trained on billions of image-text pairs from the web. It learned to encode both what things look like and what they mean by predicting which captions go with which images.

The network divides each painting into a grid of 16×16 pixel patches — like a mosaic — and processes them through 12 layers of a Vision Transformer. Early layers detect edges, textures, and color patterns. Middle layers recognize objects, faces, and spatial relationships. Late layers encode high-level concepts: "a portrait," "an interior scene," "Impressionist brushwork."

The output is a single vector of 768 numbers — a point in a 768-dimensional space. The magic is that this space is structured: paintings near each other in this space share semantic properties. A Vermeer interior and a Hopper interior are neighbors because both are "dimly lit rooms with solitary figures" — even though the paint handling, palette, and century are completely different.

This is why the SigLIP projection in the Explorer shows distinct continents for different media and traditions. Prints cluster separately from paintings because they look different AND they're described differently in the billions of web pages the model trained on.

The Iigaya model (Nature Human Behaviour, 2021) takes a fundamentally different approach. Instead of learning representations from data, it extracts 13 hand-designed features that correspond to measurable dimensions of aesthetic preference — properties that neuroscience research has shown predict how much people like a visual image.

The first eight features are computed directly from the pixels — no neural network needed. The last five use CLIP (a predecessor of SigLIP) as a binary classifier: "does this image contain people or not?", "is this concrete or abstract?", "is this dynamic or static?"

The key insight from the Iigaya research: individual aesthetic preferences decompose into weighted combinations of these 13 features. People don't just "like" or "dislike" a painting — they respond to specific perceptual dimensions, and those responses are measurable and predictable. The same features that organize visual processing in the human brain predict aesthetic preference in behavioral experiments.

What Each One Misses

SigLIP sees

768 dimensions
  • What it depicts
  • Art-historical tradition
  • Medium and technique
  • Cultural context
  • Compositional structure

Misses: the visceral — whether a painting is warm or cool, calm or agitated, heavy or light. Two paintings of the same subject in the same style can feel completely different.

Iigaya sees

13 dimensions
  • Color harmony
  • Visual weight and balance
  • Energy and movement
  • Emotional temperature
  • Perceptual complexity

Misses: the semantic — what the painting is of, who painted it, what tradition it belongs to. A Vermeer and a Hopper might score identically.

This is why the Explorer offers both projections. Switch between them and the same collection reorganizes around different axes of similarity. In the semantic view, all portraits cluster together. In the aesthetic view, dark moody portraits cluster with dark moody landscapes — because they share the same perceptual character regardless of what they depict.

What Comes Next

The Beowolff-Embed program proposes to build a single unified embedding that fuses both approaches — and adds three more signals: structured curatorial metadata (artist, period, school, medium), narrative biography (the artist's story, from Wikipedia and catalogs), and collector behavior (what people buy, save, and spend time looking at).

Five signals in one space. The embedding that results would understand not just what a painting looks like and how it feels, but what tradition it belongs to, who made it, and who will love it. That is the recommendation engine Artsy doesn't have — and the foundation of what Beowolff is building.
